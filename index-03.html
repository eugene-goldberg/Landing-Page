What problem are we solving?

The current diagnostics process

  • At the earliest stages of diagnosis, a CT scan is done for a patient
  • The initial results are first read by the radiology imaging expert, and then evaluated by the general oncologist
  • The patient gets to the “rad onc” department and meet the doctor the patient is treated with machines available at the hospital. Due to the high cost of the equipment most of the hospitals operate only a single or a few types of existing machines (the machines differ based on the technology of irradiation or on a type of radiation used).
  • The general oncologist makes some initial key decisions as to what treatment is the most effective
  • The treatment choices made by the general oncologist are influenced by: a) oncologist's current awareness of available treatment options, b) oncologist's affiliation with a specific medical facility, which, in turn, offers a very specific (and limited) number of treatment options, c) oncologist's interpretation of what a given insurance policy would cover, d) other provider-specific influencing factors (please, fill-in whatever other factors may be)
  • General oncologist creates a detailed treatment plan, which is influenced by the number of factors stated above
  • The patient is directed to a treatment facility, recommended by the primary / general oncologist's
  • At the treatment facility, a more specialized radiation oncologist performs the actual treatment, generally keeping within the guidelines outlined by the general oncologist

Problems, limitations, and their impact on the outcome

  • The initial evaluation of scan is done by a single person, and the interpretation is limited by the scope of that person’s experience
  • Only a single specific treatment method is being considered, which is influenced by the background, affiliations and practices of a single practitioner
  • The treatment plan, devised by the primary oncologist is limited to a single choice, and is influenced by the treatment facility affiliations of that particular practitioner, as well as the availability of the very specific equipment at a given single facility
  • These influencing factors provide serious limitations to the treatment options available to the patient, and may inherently cause for the less than optimal treatment to be rendered, affecting the outcomes accordingly.

Our Solution

Providence aims at getting to the patient when he/she is already indicated to get radiation but still in a phase of browsing the options.  At this moment a patient already has an imaging information and the diagnosis — the information, which can can utilized in creating the treatment plan for all existing machines and select the one that is optimal

Providence delivers highly accurate results at the early stages of cancer diagnosis, providing the patient with a near-real-time “second opinion”, produced by a highly sophisticated AI platform, which constantly improves itself with each new case

Based on the diagnostic conclusions, as well as the vast magnitude of prior learnings, Providence works out the most optimal treatment plan, which is highly tailored to a specific patient’s situation.   Multiple treatment options are being considered, and presented to the patient along with a detailed explanation of pros and cons. The AI behind Providence runs a multitude of full and detailed treatment simulations for a given patient, ultimately arriving at the most effective and least harmful approach. Based on the ultimate choice of treatment, Providence will present the patient with a list of facilities and practitioners, which offer selected treatment.
